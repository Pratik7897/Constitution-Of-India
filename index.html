Operationalising Constitutional Rights

By Soham Zagare

Two landmark Supreme Court rulings — Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017) and Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973) — are routinely discussed in law schools. What is far less discussed is how organisations are supposed to operationalise these standards.

The Proportionality Test Checklist:

  • 1. Legitimate State Aim (Is the purpose defined?)
  • 2. Rational Nexus (Does the means fit the aim?)
  • 3. Necessity (Is it the least restrictive path?)
  • 4. Proportionality Stricto Sensu (Benefit vs. Harm)

1. The Compliance Problem No One Has Solved

This article addresses the gap: what do the proportionality test and the Basic Structure Doctrine require in practice, and how do you build systems that actually comply?

2. Puttaswamy's Four-Step Proportionality Test

Puttaswamy established a four-part compliance test that any state intrusion on privacy must clear. This is not abstract philosophy. It is an auditable checklist. Every data policy that touches personal information should be stress-tested.

3. Implementation Framework

Step 1 — Aim Specification: Document in writing the exact purpose. Step 2 — Means-Ends Audit: Ask whether the same aim could be achieved with less data. Step 3 — Necessity Review: Conduct a structured review of alternatives. Step 4 — Proportionality Assessment: Quantify the expected benefit versus the privacy cost.

4. The Surveillance Gap

The 2021-22 Pegasus revelations exposed a critical implementation failure. Puttaswamy requires that any surveillance programme be legally authorised, narrowly targeted, and subject to independent oversight.

5. Kesavananda's Basic Structure

The Basic Structure Doctrine defines the irreducible architectural elements of constitutional governance. Elements like supremacy of the Constitution, separation of powers, and independence of the judiciary function as non-negotiable constraints.

6. The Electoral Bonds Case

In 2024, the Court struck down the Electoral Bonds Scheme, applying Basic Structure analysis even to ordinary legislation — confirming that protection extends beyond constitutional amendments to any law that degrades foundational values.

7. Measurable Outcomes

Impact ranges from legislative output (DPDP Act) to judicial stability across five decades. However, gaps remain in government exemptions and the lack of self-executing enforcement mechanisms.

8. Conclusion

Puttaswamy and Kesavananda define the constitutional operating environment. Closing the gap requires systematic, documented application of the analytical tools the Court has provided.

The Promise Across Time

By Nitya Gajarmal

Neither petitioner set out to make history. Justice K.S. Puttaswamy, a retired judge, and Kesavananda Bharati, head of a small mutt. Both men lost in the immediate sense — and yet the constitutional inheritance they created outlasted everything they asked for.

A Retired Judge, a Holy Man

This is what constitutional law does at its most generous: it takes a specific grievance and uses it as an occasion to ask a larger question about the relationship between the state and the people.

The Reversal That Changed Everything

Puttaswamy inverted the historical assumption. The state must now justify why it intrudes. In the constitutional scheme of India, the individual is primary; the state is the instrument.

What the Aadhaar Split Tells Us

Constitutional rights are always mediated by interpretation. The right to privacy is now constitutionally real, but its borders remain deeply contested as seen in the split 4:1 Aadhaar verdict.

Kesavananda: Refusing Betrayal

Can a document designed to outlast any government be used by a government to transform it into something unrecognisable? The answer was a narrow but firm no. Parliament may amend; it may not destroy.

The Man Who Lost, The Doctrine That Won

Kesavananda Bharati passed away in 2020. He never recovered his land. But his name is attached to a doctrine that protected democracy from the Emergency and strikes down anonymous funding today.

Why These Cases Are Not History

They are ongoing arguments. They are tools, standards, and values within which the answers to modern data governance and judicial oversight must be found.

A Final Thought

The gap between promise and delivery is filled by the courage of the judiciary and the engagement of its people. Honouring the Constitution is a democratic function that belongs to everyone.
